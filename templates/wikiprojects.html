Context

Active editors on Wikipedia nominally organize themselves using WikiProjects, gathering-places for users with similar editing interests and experiences which serve to help bind together the specific sub-populations in the Wikipedian editing community. The effectiveness of these WikiProjects, however, is highly variable. Some, like WikiProject Military history or WikiProject Video games are highly active and well-organized discussion-board-cum-communities. Some, like my home projects WikiProject Volcanoes and WikiProject Hawaii, are moderately active, but can seem lifeless at times. And some, like WikiProject Nudity (!), are graveyards.

At their best, WikiProjects help keep Wikipedians addicted: they provide friendly, even sometimes joshingly competitive spaces in which any of a number of users interested in a topic can discuss and coordinate their effects, creating, in that communal atmosphere, a reason to stick with the project. However, speaking for experience, numerically speaking by and large few WikiProjects are truly all that effective. Which really is a shame, given the prevailing nervousness about the community's health: a healthy WikiProject infastructure would help keep the community more cohesive. WikiProject X is an ongoing program led by a few experienced Wikipedians that is attempting to proactively refresh WikiProjects.

I now ranked the WikiProjects and plotted them according to size:

Here when I mean size I mean the number of articles which the WikiProject covers. Large WikiProjects like the Biography or Military history one have a very, very, very broad basis of coverage; more narrow ones, like WikiProject Hawaii or WikiProject Seamounts, have a much narrower scope.

The exponential distribution of WikiProject sizes is abundantly obvious: there are a few very, very large WikiProjects which dominate everyone else. Exponential curves like this one are far and away the norm amongst measures of online communities: academic analysis has demonstrated that almost all social networks follow something Zipf-like at scale. This graph on its own is not very informative, however. We can get a better sense of what's going on by looking at the log-log plot:

Here you can see that WikiProject sizes are not at all log-normal: indeed, ignoring failed WikiProjects at the tip of the distribution the majority of WikiProjects fit into a prominent "bow" of larger-than-expected projects.

Just from a cursory examination of this list you can already get a sense of the biases at play on Wikipedia. Put yourself in the midset of your typical Wikipedian: according to a 2011 Editor Survey you are overwhelmingly white, English-speaking (duh), male, and kind of a nerd (don't knock it 'til you try it!). What types of articles do you edit: ones on your local highway in Alberta or ones on a village in Africa? The beauty of Wikipedia is that it is such a comprehensive resource, covering a wider breadth of information than any other resource in existance. Its curse, though, is that, yes, an entire continent (Africa) gets less than a fourth of coverage that the United States does, and only two thirds that of Australia, a country with on the order of a fifthieth of the population.

Correlative strength

Categorization was one of the most important meta-tasks in the early history of Wikipedia: Wikipedians then as now were very much interested in the how much and of what of their editing areas. Today the vast majority of Wikipedia articles are categorized according to WikiProject, most according to multiple ones: Shield volcano, for instance, is associated with WikiProjects in Volcanoes and Geology, while Barrack Obama has something like a dozen of them. I was interested in gauging the relative levels of overlap in content coverage for various WikiProjects. How often WikiProject Geology editors interact with WikiProject Mountains ones? How about WikiProject Alaska and WikiProject Glaciers?

However, this turned out to be a hard question to answer: not because the necessary queries were difficult to write, but because the raw volume of API requests necessary for thorough classification are out of immediate reach. I measured correlation by sampling a WikiProject's pages and then parsing the templates on their talk pages for connections to alternative WikiProjects. After playing around with the data for some time I realized that to construct a meaingful dataset this way I would need to analyze the top 500 or so projects: a task which would require, the way the code is currently written, over a hundred thousand API queries. This is simply too much scale for me to manage for the moment.
