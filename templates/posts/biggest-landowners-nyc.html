An important and oftentimes conscientious aspect of a city’s social fabric is the way in which it houses its residents; you can learn a lot about a city from its real estate, and nowhere is this more true than it is in New York City.

In this post I used data from the New York City Department of Planning's NYC PLUTO dataset, a massive dataset containing building-granularity information on every so-called "Borough Block Lot" identifier in New York City, and the RPAD dataset, the New York City Department of Finance's record of property value assessments, to try to answer a basic question that's proven to be very popular in the media: who are the biggest landowners in New York City?

Counting Addresses

If by "largest" you mean "owner of the most unique addresses" (on further consideration a pretty terrible metric, but nevertheless an interesting place to start), here are the top ten raw winners:

Somewhat unsurprisingly the single largest property-holder in New York City is city government, with various agencies taking all but one of the top 10 and most of the top 50 slots. The sole exception is sixth biggest property-owner OWNER / AGENT—a mildly alarming instance of indeterminate ownership in the public record. There is also an option for the 33 properties in the city for whom an owner is apparently simply UNKNOWN.

However, the public record of public asset ownership is a little haphazard. In additional to dedicated points for each of the individual city agenices there are several variations on "the City of New York" (CITY OF NEW YORK, THE CITY OF NEW YORK, etc.), and a similar situation exists for federal and state agencies, which are listed either individually or as part of the UNITED STATES GOVERNMENT, NEW YORK STATE, or even THE PEOPLE OF THE STATE OF NEW YORK. To explore the raw data yourself, check out this Gist.

It's easier for us to examine private entity data. A Q&A published in The New York Times in 1994 fingers Columbia University, NYU, Trinity Church, and a handful of developers as the biggest in terms of private addresses held. What does the picture look like today? Filtering out public services, here is what the public record has to say about the 20 private owners with the largest number of buildings:

Why aren't there more, or indeed, almost any, large corporations on the listing? Because even though many companies call New York City their home, either or both in workforce and in headquarters, corporations own very little of the land they use. Companies strongly prefer renting or leasing their space instead. Wells Fargo is seemingly the largest casually recognizable corporate landowner in New York City—yet it owns just 52 locations, a far cry from the hundred-fold branches that it operates.

Summing Land Ownership

Another measure of "largest" is "owner of the most building area". This probably gets closer to the intent of the question, and so, using that metric, once again here is our top ten:

The New York City Housing Authority and the Department of Education are easily the largest landowners in New York City. Interestingly this means that there is 30% more affordable housing than public school to the city.

If we combine "United States Of America" with all of the US Government sub-entities in the dataset—the National Park Service, USPS, etc.—it is probably the third largest landowner in the city. The National Park Service on its own is fourth largest.
